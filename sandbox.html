The Privacy Sandbox: Google's Attempt to Retire the Cookie

The biggest recent chapter in cookie history is Google's long and still unfinished attempt to get rid of the third party cookie. Apple's Safari and Mozilla's Firefox started blocking third party cookies by default years ago, unlike Chrome, which handles more than half of all web browsing worldwide and kept allowing them, since Google's own advertising business depends on them so much.

In 2019, Google launched the Privacy Sandbox, a set of proposed replacement technologies like the Topics API and the Protected Audience API, meant to support ad targeting and measurement without tracking individual people, along with plans to fully remove third party cookies from Chrome by 2022.

Why the Plan Kept Getting Delayed

  • The removal date got pushed back over and over through 2022, 2023, and 2024.
  • Antitrust regulators, including the UK's Competition and Markets Authority and its Information Commissioner's Office, worried Google's replacement system would just deepen its own dominance over digital advertising.
  • Advertisers and publishers reported revenue drops of 20 percent or more during early tests without cookies.

Where It Landed

In July 2024, Google dropped the full removal plan in favor of a simpler user choice prompt, then in April 2025 it dropped that prompt too, leaving cookie controls inside Chrome's regular settings menu instead. By October 2025, Google confirmed it was retiring most of the original Privacy Sandbox APIs altogether because of low adoption across the industry, while keeping a few smaller tools like CHIPS, for partitioning cookies by site, and FedCM, for safer logins.

So the third party cookie did not die on a set deadline like Google originally planned. It just got left in place but pushed further to the side by consent rules, browser settings, and fewer users choosing to allow it in the first place.
