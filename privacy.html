The Privacy Problem

Once cookies were proven useful, the Internet Engineering Task Force put together a working group, led by Bell Labs researcher David Kristol and Montulli himself, to turn Netscape's informal design into an actual open standard. As early as 1996, the group was already worried about "third party" cookies, meaning cookies set not by the site someone is actually visiting, but by an advertising script embedded inside that site, which lets the ad company recognize the same browser across a bunch of different, unrelated websites.

The group's first draft tried to block third party cookies by default. A Dutch member of the group named Koen Holtman warned early on that ad companies could just agree to share cookies across networks of sites to get around the rule anyway. Advertising networks pushed back on the restriction, and by the time the standard published as RFC 2109 in 1997, the rule had been watered down into more of a suggestion than an actual requirement. Netscape and Internet Explorer both shipped with third party cookies allowed by default, and a whole industry of cross site tracking and behavioral advertising grew on top of that one decision over the next twenty years.

First Party vs Third Party Cookies

First Party Cookie Third Party Cookie
Set by the site you are actually visiting Set by an outside script embedded in the site, usually an ad network
Keeps you logged in and remembers your cart Builds a profile of your activity across many unrelated sites
Generally seen as necessary for a site to work The main target of privacy laws and browser blocking

Where the Laws Came From

Public concern from investigative journalism and research linking cookies to behavioral tracking led directly to new laws, starting with the EU's 2009 ePrivacy Directive, which required sites to get consent before setting non essential cookies. The EU's 2018 GDPR added strict rules on personal data in general, and California's CCPA and a bunch of other United States state privacy laws followed after that. The cookie consent banner that pops up on almost every website today is the direct and very visible result of those laws.
